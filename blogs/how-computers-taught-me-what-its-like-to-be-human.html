How computers taught me what it's like to be human

Published on July 19, 2026 — Written by Farouk Ashraf

First contanct!

I still remember the smell of that first computer I've ever owned. It was the family computer that dad had bought for me and my brother. I must have been no older than 8 or 9 at the time. I initially only wanted to play with it and viewed it as a toy or rather a whole store's worth of games wrapped in one. I've always wanted a computer since I was 6 years old. I used to wander with the mind of a child about how I could make my own computer out of wires and boxes and E-waste that layed all over the house.

Over time, I grew to understand what the computer really was. When I got online for the first time, it was a rather transformative experience. Looking up everything I've ever been curious about and finding the answer right in front of my eyes. It was a strange feeling. I realized then that the computer is not a toy. It's a platform through which I could experience things. Things I couldn't describe at the time because I had no words for them. Concepts I wouldn't have came to understand on my own.

Natural curiousity

Over time I started getting dragged into early youtube where I only found the worst kinds of predatory content waiting for me. Conspirancy theories. Imagine what listening to people a hundred times crazier than Alex Johnes could do to such a young mind. I fractured under the pressure resulting from the explosion of information poring into my brain and reshaping my neural pathways both in good and bad ways. Thankfully I would discover other kinds of content as I grew older. What I like to call STEMtube changed my life. Before I knew it I went from learning all sorts of falsehoods about modern life from consipracy cranks to slowly understanding the natural world and my place in it. A few months would pass and I would go so deep down the rabbithole that I was trying to learn advanced mathematics because I wanted to understand string theory in all its glory. Yes, that string theory. Crazy world.. right?

I wouldn't keep up with physics of course because life had naturally taken its course and my teenager brain would discover other hobbies it would enjoy very much. Nothing was as impactful to my life as coding and development. Before I hit 13 I was already coding websites, small forums that no one used. I wrote blogs that no one read. Poetry. Articles. You name it. I made videos nobody would see. It didn't matter that I wasn't famous. I was happy..

My hardware enthusiast phase

Around 15, I was pretty convinced it was time to build a proper computer that could keep up with the times. I would spend years researching every component of every PC build I came across. I could've ran a hardware store at that age and I would've been remarkably successful hahaha. I was obsessed with overclocking like everyone was at the time. I actually upgraded my celeron to a core 2 duo a few years later and somehow would overclock it on my old motherboard. It was far from a stable overclock, I would crash all the time, but it didn't matter. I was happy. It was then that I had switched to linux long term for the first time. Turned that old family PC into a media PC that I would keep in my room to watch movies on. It was a pretty smart idea. I eventually moved to new appartment with my family and we'd forget about that PC completely because it got too slow to be of any use to anyone other than me.

I still had a laptop and that lasted me a couple years before I had to upgrade because the battery failed then the trackpad, then the keys from the keyboard started to commit seppuku whenever I'd type a little too fast. It was then that I'd consistantly experiment with FOSS software and I would find my calling when I switch to linux once every few months and learn a new trick. The tricks accumulated and my brain was slowly rewired in a way I dind't understand just yet.

How Linux reshaped my brain

I would notice these patterns over the years. First it started with my willingness to root my phone and then use a custom rom the first time I used linux. I thought if I could install a custom OS for my laptop, why not my phone? Why not change my environment to suit me? Something clicked in my 13 year old brain then. I had an entire world at my fingertips where I could scream, laugh, cry, pain, sing and no soul would notice or judge. Everytime I edited a config file and saw the changes reflected before my eyes I would grow even more attached to the virtual space I was building. My own world. My own algorithms. My own friends who think and talk like me. It was bliss.

Then I grew older. Went to college, I would always try to impress people with the crazy stuff I'd make but very few would ever understand, and that's okay. Because FOSS taught me to be independant. To think for myself even if everyone thought I was wasting my time looking over code before running it blindly.

Truth is, I was practicing the famous "trust but verify". I was growing more cautious the more I learned about cybersecurity and privacy and the insane world of data brokering. Little did I know that all the little nudges linux and open source gave me would come back to help me years later. Every source of knowledge to me was the Arch Wiki which I would read religiously. I understood how to do my research before nuking my system with a troll command. I guess it was always software that guided me to be more responsible with my digital self, and my environment. I wouldn't litter in the street in the same way I understood why it sucks to clean up useless junk that's filling up my SSD. I wouldn't go into an exam without at least reading a few parts of the "wiki" because no one in their right mind goes to troubleshoot without their journalctl. I cook my scripts and programs in the same way I cook my food. Functional.

What about you fellas? How did computers affect you?
